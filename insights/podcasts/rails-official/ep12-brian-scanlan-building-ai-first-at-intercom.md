# Brian Scanlan: Building AI-First at Intercom

Brian Scanlan (senior principal engineer at Intercom, on its platform group) describes turning Claude Code into a full-stack engineering platform on top of Intercom's 15-year-old B2B SaaS Rails monolith of millions of lines — 100+ internal skills, hooks that enforce engineering workflows, and read-only production access used by engineers, PMs, support, and design alike.

## The Monolith and "Technically Conservative"

- Intercom is a 15-year-old B2B SaaS with millions of lines of code, most in the Rails app; breaking it into microservices is not the goal
- Rails is prized for expressiveness, ease of getting started, and letting product engineers focus on product instead of framework/boilerplate/microservice complexity
- Guiding principle: **"technically conservative"** — the org's articulation of what has served it for 15 years; invest deeply in a small set of well-understood technologies
- Explicit debt to Dan McKinley's _Choose Boring Technology_ (ex-Etsy): bespoke tech is "like owning a pet" — you must feed and maintain it; boring, shared components get scaled by the collective effort
- "It's hard enough to scale one database" — picking different databases per team would be unmanageable
- The platform group owns availability, performance, cost, security, and developer productivity — from AWS accounts up to owning and writing Rails code, plus Rails upgrades, observability, and query/database optimization
- No DBAs or sysadmins on staff; teams are staffed with **generalist product engineers** who go deep in niches — they deliberately do not hire for specialist Rails-only backgrounds

## CI "Melting" Under AI-Driven Throughput

- Rails monolith test suite is **hundreds of thousands of tests**, ~2.5 days of wall-clock time fully serialized; highly parallelized to ship up to **100 times a day**
- Inability to ship is treated as a **P1 event** — "if we can't ship, we're down"
- Two failure modes emerged as PR throughput rose (starting ~summer): a rising tide of **flaky tests** tripping over each other, and CI **cost growing faster than the (fast-growing) business** — nonlinear cost growth is what "blew up"
- Uses Shopify's **Rotoscope** to run a subset of tests per change — saves compute/cost but not wall-clock time (the slowest test still dictates duration); higher velocity + Rotoscope increased retries and flakiness to a critical mass
- Any failing test is automatically retried; a genuinely flaky test fails on one seed/order and passes on the next — tolerable only while shipping constantly
- **No single magic fix.** Applied the boring scientific method: used Honeycomb deep tracing to find bottlenecks, then dozens of individual fixes
- Root cause was largely in **factories** — heavyweight "semi-god" objects (conversations, workspaces) with expensive setup invoked hundreds of thousands of times; fix was lightweight variants that only do the complex setup when actually needed
- Also resized hosts and temporarily **stopped using spot instances** to buy stability (spot hosts are cheap but preemptible — fine for reruns if tuned so a host signaling shutdown stops picking up new tests)

## Making the Codebase Agent-Friendly

- CTO Dara set a goal to **double R&D throughput in a year**, measured crudely by **pull requests per head** — met (and passed) it ~2 weeks before recording; next target is to double again, with 10x seen as realistic
- Principle: **all technical work is becoming agent-first** (mirroring Greg Brockman's OpenAI stance) — if you're not opening an agent first to do a piece of work (incident, planning, research, code), you're doing it wrong
- **Over 90–95%** (some days higher) of code changed in the Rails codebase each day is generated by Claude Code; the vision is engineers not reading code — "shut down your IDEs, tell Claude your problems"
- **"Good for humans is good for LLMs"** — docs, tests, short pieces of code all help both; expanded RuboCop coverage and more sophisticated cops (Claude writes decent cops) as guardrails so AI doesn't produce code "inspired by code we wrote 15 years ago"
- Rather than refactor the whole codebase, **direct guidance beats boiling the ocean**: e.g. 10 HTTP client wrappers existed; consolidation "petered out" and was hard to prove impactful, so they left patterns in place and just tell Claude which client to use and to avoid metaprogramming
- Not everything is factory work: the "software factory" produces IKEA-style repeatable output, but **artisan/ornate/novel work** and pixel-precise front-end/UI work still keep a human and editor in the loop

## Automated Code Review and PR Approval

- **Code review is the current bottleneck.** Goal: the majority of Rails PRs approved with **no human approver**; today ~**20% of Rails-app PRs are auto-approved** by a Claude-Code-based "safe PR" system, targeting >50%
- **Dependabot** PRs are auto-merged (no human) in batches on Tue/Wed/Thu at set times, visible in a Slack channel; a small set of **load-bearing dependencies is opted out** (Rails itself, the AWS gem, LangChain in Python) where a human stays in the loop
- Deterministic, path-based auto-approvals: spec-only changes, documentation changes, etc. ship without approval
- **SOC 2 / ISO 27001 do not require humans to approve each other's work** — they require documented process, controls, mitigations, and audit trails, all achievable with automated review
- Building the reviewer: naive "review this code" produced reviews that *looked* good but were noisy and "psychopathic"; fix was to "**put manners on the bots**" — don't comment unless highly impactful, keep it short, say nothing if nothing to say, plus curated good/bad examples
- Built a **feedback loop with real data**: generated candidate reviews over historical changes, had the best Rails engineers grade them against their own reviews, and tuned until engineers acted on the feedback
- Approval criteria (encoded so an agent can judge "is this safe?"): the "perfect change" is not hundreds of lines, is behind a feature flag where possible, and doesn't touch hot/critical code paths — which can trigger an **"ejector seat"** forcing a human in
- Oversight is shifting from blocking to **reactive**: weekly agent-generated summaries of changes in a team's area replace gatekeeping; a **deployment agent** will monitor 100% of deploys the way your best engineer would watch a Rails upgrade

## Skills, Rules, Hooks, and Plugins

- **Claude Code is mandated as the single tool** — you must pick one platform to build repeatable, sophisticated skills on top of; **over 1,000 weekly Claude Code users** across Intercom
- Tiered plugin architecture:
  - **Base plugin** — telemetry + safety for everyone; hooks send per-skill/per-session metadata to Honeycomb (so anyone can explore skill usage), and full Claude Code session JSON is copied (lightly anonymized) to **S3** for quality control and mining "gold"
  - **Core developer plugins** — cross-Intercom functions (fix a flaky spec, open a PR); very high bar, must have **evals**, adhere to Anthropic's skill guide plus internal skill guides; skills "graduate" into this tier and are committed to being maintained
  - **Team-specific plugins** — lower quality bar, more opinionated, overfit to a team's workflow; some are high quality but deliberately hidden (e.g. the **Rails upgrade skill**, restricted to one team so people don't accidentally upgrade Rails apps)
  - **Long tail** — personal skills; liberal policy of "let many flowers grow," then mine session data to pick the best
- Belief in **small, discrete, testable skills** — monolithic skills that do many things are bad; small skills can be assembled into larger flows; happy to have ~10 overlapping "investigate a bug" skills and extract the good parts
- Different levers for different needs: **skills, rules, guidance, hooks**. Hooks can **force a behavior** when a rule isn't reliably invoked
- Example: a PR-description skill — out of the box Claude "regurgitates" what the code does (useless); the real value is the **why**. A rule calls a skill that inspects session data to establish purpose and asks the person if it can't; when the rule wasn't consistently honored, they promoted it to a **hook**
- Evals prove whether a behavior happens as intended; telemetry + asking users why a skill wasn't invoked (wrong Claude Code version? wrong plugin version?) drives "a huge tech-support kind of thing"
- Plugins/config are distributed via **internal IT systems**, and Claude is locked down in certain ways — though nothing stops someone downloading Codex and bypassing it; the aim is a **safer default**, not to eliminate shadow IT

## Deterministic vs. Open-Ended: Where Agents Flail

- Claude Code excels at **deterministic, well-understood jobs** (Rails upgrades, fixing flaky tests) — roughly 90% solved — but "really struggles with very open-ended problems"
- Incident cautionary tale: an SLO alarm (tracking Intercom inbox reply activity) fired on a weekend; Claude produced **plausible-but-wrong** root causes and led a very experienced engineer down multiple wrong paths, settling on one. Actual cause was an **eventual-consistency issue in Datadog metrics** — non-obvious
- "It is worse to be brought down a load of red herrings than to have Claude admit 'I have no idea what happened'"
- Guardrail approach: encode each novel edge case once so Claude checks it every time, eventually covering ~99% of edge cases
- **Commercial-airline-pilot metaphor**: pilots land on autopilot most of the time but stay tested in the simulator and take manual control periodically to keep sharp. Even when Claude can resolve outages faster than a human, Brian intends to "race it" and compare — "if you're a factory owner, you need to deeply understand the quality of the output"
- Brian joins **every incident channel** ("the eye of Sauron") as a human sanity check on agent conclusions

## Read-Only Production Access and the Rails Console MCP

- Core philosophy: **"anything you can do on your laptop, your agent should be able to do."** There's "no harm in a read-only view into the entirety of your production environment" — so give everyone the ability to point Claude at read-only production
- Origin story: Brian was manually pasting Rails console output back to Claude and realized he was acting as a proxy — so he built an **MCP that lets Claude Code run arbitrary Ruby against the production Rails console over API**
- The MCP takes an injected string (can be a multi-part function/lambda-like payload), runs safety checks against it, executes it, reports output, and **logs to an audit trail**; it runs in the production environment (same as SSHing in and running `bin/rails console`), and builds on existing console safeguards (e.g. `Console1984`-style safety)
- Currently **read-only** (no mutation) by design; writes are intentionally kept to a purpose-built API endpoint — "if it's important enough to consistently write, build an API endpoint," giving a more predictable, validated surface
- Less ergonomic than a real REPL (no tab-complete, likely a different server each call, must get it right first time) — "it gets it wrong many times… but when it gets things right, it's amazing"
- Context comes from **guidance/skill hints plus the codebase itself** — users are usually inside the Rails repo, and Claude interleaves "explore the codebase → run in production → explore → run"
- Killer result: the **top 5 users of the Rails console MCP were all non-engineers** — design managers, senior directors of product, support — unwittingly running complex Rails REPL code because Claude does it for them, work they'd never have gone to the console to do

## Beyond Engineering: Democratized Data Access

- Writing code "might be the least important thing" Claude Code does at Intercom — the biggest non-R&D use case is **ad-hoc analytics** over data in Snowflake
- "Democratized" data access: instead of building/maintaining dashboards, non-technical staff ask questions directly and get answers first-try with the right guardrails — making many dashboards effectively **obsolete**
- Viral, bottoms-up adoption across sales, marketing, support; enablers were **one mandated tool, good Snowflake data controls, great MCP configs, and heavy technical support**
- Non-engineers are learning the terminal because they see colleagues' productivity — Intercom's VP of Operations even built a "getting started with the terminal" guide/app with Claude Code
- Integration is mostly **standalone/local MCPs per service** (auth to many things), with ongoing consolidation of internal admin tools into the Rails app behind APIs

## Security, Cost, and the Agent-First Future

- Read-only console access doesn't change *what* data people can access (they already had it) — but it **increases volume and surface area**, so **detection matters more**; more laptops now hold more active credentials
- Securing LLMs is "very much an unsolved problem" — governance over which MCPs/plugins people connect to reduces "garbage in," control the inputs/harness to control the outputs
- **Token cost stance**: not optimizing token consumption right now — the **opportunity cost of caring is too high**; spend "is not sustainable" long-term but worth it to stay on the bleeding edge. Guardrails against sloppiness: don't use Opus where Sonnet suffices, watch Playwright token burn, avoid needlessly complex orchestration
- Treat AI spend as coming out of **headcount budget**, not a per-seat license line item; Brian primed finance a year early with Steve Yegge's "Death of the Junior Developer" prediction of 10–100x token growth. If budget bureaucracy blocks adoption, escalate to C-level
- **Ruby/Rails + LLMs**: 2 years ago results were worse for Rails (esp. custom/large-codebase Rails) than React/Node/Python; something changed last year — **harnesses got much better at large codebases and discovery**, and Claude now writes idiomatic Rails, refactors to idiomatic Ruby on request, and doesn't go down wrong paths. Sorbet or unusually structured codebases *might* still be a barrier
- **Wishlist for the Rails ecosystem — "Agent Experience (AX)" / "Agent EO"**: make the zero-to-one experience agent-first so LLMs default to picking Rails (they currently default to Node/React); native or well-supported gems for agent-safe ephemeral code execution and a console/CLI surface (like a Heroku CLI for agents) so he wouldn't have to hand-build the console API; reassess convention-over-configuration assuming **agents, not people, drive the setup**
- Make docs **agent-consumable**: markdown-accessible developer docs, fast search, fewer steps, transcripts/summaries instead of videos — fewer tokens/steps behaves like lower latency and raises "conversion" of agents choosing your tool
- Book recommendation: _Designing Data-Intensive Applications_ (Martin Kleppmann) — reread the classics to stay fresh on fundamentals
